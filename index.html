The Mathematics of Morphogenesis

In 1952, Alan Turing published "The Chemical Basis of Morphogenesis," proposing that patterns in biology — spots, stripes, spirals — arise from the interaction of just two chemicals (morphogens) diffusing and reacting across a tissue.

The Gray-Scott Model

The Gray-Scott model is a specific reaction-diffusion system with two chemical species U and V:

du/dt = Du nabla2u - uv2 + F(1 - u)
dv/dt = Dv nabla2v + uv2 - (F + k)v

Where:

Du, Dv — Diffusion Rates

How fast each chemical spreads through space. The key insight: U diffuses faster than V (activator-inhibitor pattern). This differential diffusion is what creates instability and pattern formation.

F — Feed Rate

Rate at which chemical U is replenished from an external source (like nutrients flowing into a cell culture). Controls how "alive" the system is.

k — Kill Rate

Rate at which chemical V is removed from the system (like degradation or consumption). Controls pattern density and type.

uv2 — Autocatalytic Reaction

The cubic reaction term: V catalyzes its own production by consuming U. This positive feedback loop is the "activator" that creates local peaks of concentration.

Turing Instability

The magic happens when a uniform steady state becomes unstable to spatial perturbations. Linearizing around the steady state and analyzing the dispersion relation reveals that certain spatial frequencies grow exponentially — these become the pattern wavelength.

Dispersion relation: sigma(q) = trace(J)/2 +/- sqrt((trace(J)/2)2 - det(J(q)))
Pattern forms when sigma > 0 for some wavenumber q != 0 while sigma(0) < 0

This means the system is stable to uniform changes but unstable to spatial ones — the hallmark of a Turing pattern.

Connection to Biology

Zebrafish Stripes

Melanophores (dark) and xanthophores (light) interact through direct cell contact and diffusible signals, creating Turing patterns. Shoji et al. (2003) showed the reaction-diffusion model accurately predicts stripe formation and mutation effects.

Leopard Spots

Morphogen gradients (likely involving Wnt and DKK4 pathways) create periodic patterns during hair follicle development. The spots emerge during embryonic development and are "frozen in" as the animal grows.

Coral Growth

Branching coral morphology can be modeled as reaction-diffusion on a growing surface. The Laplacian instability at the growth front, combined with nutrient diffusion, creates fractal-like branching patterns.

Digit Formation

The spacing of fingers and toes in vertebrates is governed by a Turing-type mechanism involving BMP, SOX9, and Wnt signaling pathways — confirmed experimentally by Raspopovic et al. (2014) in mouse limb buds.

Neural Cellular Automata

NCA modernize Turing's vision using neural networks. Instead of hand-designed reaction terms, a small neural network learns the local update rule from data. Each cell:

1. Perceives neighbors (via Sobel filters: identity, gradient_x, gradient_y)
2. Processes perception through a neural network (Conv1x1 -> ReLU -> Conv1x1)
3. Applies a stochastic update (dropout mask for asynchronous updates)
4. Result: state[t+1] = state[t] + update * mask

The residual connection + stochastic updates enable robust self-organization and self-repair — properties that emerge from the training process rather than being explicitly programmed.
